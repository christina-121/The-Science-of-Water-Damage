        <!-- Introduction -->
        <p><strong>Water intrusion is not just a cosmetic issue — it is a structural one.</strong> When moisture enters a building, it interacts with materials in predictable, often destructive ways. Understanding the science of how water affects wood, drywall, insulation, and other components is essential for recognizing the urgency of proper extraction and drying. This guide explains the physical and chemical processes behind water damage, the role of capillary action, and why hidden moisture is one of the most dangerous threats to your property.</p>

        <!-- Section 1: Materials & Moisture -->
        <h2>🧱 1. How Different Materials React to Moisture</h2>
        <p>Every building material responds to water differently. Some absorb and swell, others soften and degrade, and some provide a food source for mold. The severity of damage depends on the material type, the duration of exposure, and whether the water is clean, gray, or black (contaminated).</p>

        <div class="grid-2">
            <div class="card">
                <div class="icon-large">🪵</div>
                <h3>Wood</h3>
                <p>Wood is hygroscopic — it absorbs moisture from the air and surrounding materials. When wet, it swells, warps, and becomes a prime substrate for mold and rot. Prolonged exposure leads to structural weakening and decay.</p>
            </div>
            <div class="card">
                <div class="icon-large">🧱</div>
                <h3>Drywall (Gypsum)</h3>
                <p>Drywall absorbs water quickly, losing structural integrity. The paper facing feeds mold, and the gypsum core can become soft and crumble. Even minor water exposure often requires replacement.</p>
            </div>
            <div class="card">
                <div class="icon-large">🧵</div>
                <h3>Insulation</h3>
                <p>Fiberglass insulation loses its R-value when wet and can trap moisture against wood framing. Spray foam can become a moisture barrier, but cellulose insulation absorbs water like a sponge, promoting mold and rot.</p>
            </div>
            <div class="card">
                <div class="icon-large">🏗️</div>
                <h3>Concrete & Masonry</h3>
                <p>Concrete is porous and can wick moisture upward via capillary action. While it doesn't rot, it can cause efflorescence, spalling, and corrosion of embedded steel reinforcement.</p>
            </div>
        </div>

        <!-- Section 2: Capillary Action -->
        <h2>⬆️ 2. Capillary Action: How Water Defies Gravity</h2>
        <p>Capillary action is the ability of a liquid to flow in narrow spaces without the assistance of external forces like gravity. In buildings, water can rise through tiny pores in concrete, masonry, and wood. This is why water can appear high up a wall even when the source is at ground level. Capillary action explains how moisture spreads laterally and vertically, often traveling far from the original source.</p>
        <div class="blink-info">
            <span style="font-size:1.8rem;">📈</span> <strong>Critical Fact:</strong> Water can wick up to 3 meters through porous materials via capillary action, creating hidden damage far from the visible water source.
        </div>

        <!-- Section 3: Swelling, Rot & Structural Weakening -->
        <h2>🌀 3. Swelling, Rot, and Structural Weakening</h2>
        <p>When wood absorbs water, its fibers swell. Repeated cycles of wetting and drying cause micro-fractures, leading to warping, splitting, and loss of load-bearing capacity. If moisture persists, rot fungi (e.g., <em>Serpula lacrymans</em>) break down the cellulose and lignin, causing brown or white rot. The result: floors become spongy, beams sag, and walls lose their ability to support loads.</p>

        <!-- Section 4: Hidden Water -->
        <h2>🕵️ 4. Hidden Water: The Invisible Threat</h2>
        <p>Water does not always leave visible stains immediately. It can travel through wall cavities, along pipes, and into subfloors where it remains undetected for weeks or months. This hidden moisture creates the perfect environment for mold and decay. Moisture meters and thermal imaging are used by professionals to detect moisture below surfaces. By the time visible signs appear (stains, bubbling paint, musty odors), the damage may already be extensive.</p>

        <!-- Section 5: The Extraction and Drying Process -->
        <h2>💨 5. Proper Extraction and Drying: The Restoration Imperative</h2>
        <p>Effective restoration follows a scientific approach: extraction, evaporation, and dehumidification. Professional-grade equipment — such as truck-mounted extractors, air movers, and desiccant dehumidifiers — is used to remove water and lower the moisture content of materials to safe levels. In New Orleans, where humidity is high, this process requires specialized knowledge and equipment to prevent secondary damage like mold growth and warping.</p>
        <div class="blink-info">
            <span style="font-size:1.8rem;">⏳</span> <strong>Time is the Enemy:</strong> In New Orleans' climate, mold can start growing within 24–48 hours of water exposure. Rapid, professional drying is not just a convenience — it is a necessity to prevent irreversible damage.
        </div>

        <!-- Section 6: Why New Orleans Is a Special Case -->
        <h2>🌧️ 6. New Orleans: A High-Risk Environment for Water Damage</h2>
        <p>New Orleans' combination of below-sea-level elevation, 60+ inches of annual rainfall, and high humidity makes it one of the most water-damage-prone cities in the U.S. The aging building stock — many homes and commercial properties are 50–100+ years old — adds to the vulnerability. Property owners in New Orleans must be especially vigilant about early detection, rapid response, and professional restoration to protect their investments.</p>

        <!-- Section 7: Protecting Your Property -->
        <h2>🛡️ 7. Protecting Your Property: Prevention and Action</h2>
        <ul style="margin-left: 1.6rem; margin-bottom: 1.5rem;">
            <li>🔍 <strong>Regular inspections:</strong> Check roofs, gutters, basements, and crawl spaces for signs of moisture.</li>
            <li>💧 <strong>Monitor humidity:</strong> Use dehumidifiers to keep indoor relative humidity below 60%.</li>
            <li>🧫 <strong>Address leaks promptly:</strong> Even a small drip can lead to significant hidden damage.</li>
            <li>📋 <strong>Know your insurance:</strong> Ensure flood and water damage are adequately covered.</li>
            <li>📞 <strong>Partner with a certified restoration professional:</strong> Early intervention can save thousands.</li>
        </ul>

        <!-- Final Summary -->
        <div class="blink-info">
            <span style="font-size:1.8rem;">🎯</span> <strong>Key Takeaway:</strong> Water damage is a scientific process — it follows predictable patterns of absorption, wicking, and material degradation. Understanding these principles helps property owners recognize the urgency of professional extraction and drying, and why waiting is the most expensive choice.
        </div>

        <p><strong>Summary:</strong> Moisture is one of the most destructive forces in any building. From wood rot to mold growth to structural weakening, the effects of water intrusion are often hidden until significant damage has occurred. By understanding how different materials react, the role of capillary action, and the importance of rapid, professional drying, property owners can make informed decisions that protect both their health and their investment. In high-risk areas like New Orleans, this knowledge is essential.</p>
